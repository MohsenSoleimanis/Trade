UX Audit · v3 — Information Architecture

One workspace. Zero tab tours.

Audit of how a person actually moves through De Waag today, and the redesign: the entire trading loop — see, judge, act, monitor — lives on one screen. Grounded in the ui-ux-pro-max navigation rules and how professional terminals are actually laid out.

Part 1 — The UX audit: walk one real task

The only honest way to audit UX: follow a real user doing a real thing. Task: "the engine suggested Colruyt — decide and act on it." Today's journey:

StepWhere the user must goWhat's lost on the way
1Today tab — sees the brief mentioning COLR—
2Autopilot tab — reads the narrated proposalthe brief's context is gone
3Library → Dossiers → COLR — checks chart & fundamentalsthe proposal's numbers are gone; user must remember them
4Pipeline tab — the idea card lives here, thesis is written herethe dossier is gone again
5Back to dossier or Library → Desk — place the orderthe thesis text isn't visible while ordering
6Today or Library → Risk — see the position and its riskeverything again
Finding 1 · the navigation mirrors the code, not the person 10 destinations (4 tabs + 6 sub-tabs) exist because the backend has modules. The user's single task crossed 6 of them, losing context at every hop. Every hop = re-orientation = cognitive tax. This is why it "doesn't feel like one product".
Finding 2 · the same entity is scattered "Colruyt" exists in five places (screener row, pipeline card, dossier, desk ticket, position line) — and none of them show the others. The skill's rule: state-preservation — context must never be lost between related views.
Finding 3 · mixed navigation patterns Top tabs + inner sub-tab chips + back-links = the skill's avoid-mixed-patterns anti-pattern. Primary and secondary navigation aren't separated (nav-hierarchy).
Finding 4 · the acting surface is far from the deciding surface You research in one place and act in another. Professional terminals never separate these — chart and order ticket share a screen because the decision and the action are one motion.

Part 2 — The redesign: one workspace, three zones

The organizing principle LEFT = everything you could look at (markets, your book, your ideas — one list, three lenses). CENTER = the one thing you're looking at now. RIGHT = acting and being alerted. The loop browse → examine → act → monitor becomes eye movement, not navigation. Skill rules applied: adaptive-navigation (persistent sidebar ≥1024px), persistent-nav, navigation-consistency, content-priority, one primary CTA per screen.

The whole app on one screen. Click any name on the left — the center becomes it instantly, the ACT panel re-targets to it, and nothing else is lost. The pipeline is no longer a place you go — it's the status tags on your ideas and the Thesis tab in the stage. Autopilot is no longer a page — its proposals are feed cards that open in the stage with the trade pre-staged. The screener is the Markets lens of the left rail.
