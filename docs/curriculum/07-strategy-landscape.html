The Strategy Landscape: How People Actually Beat Markets

You now know what prices are, how trading works, how to read and value a company, what moves the tide, and how to survive being wrong. Time for the map: the handful of documented strategy families that have beaten markets, why they work, what each one costs to hold — and which ones fit a person in Belgium with a day job.

1. The baseline nobody beats by accident

The idea in one sentenceThe default is the broad ETF at ~7%/year for zero effort — every strategy is a claim to beat it, and every claim must explain who is paying you and why.

Lesson 1 gave you the three-item list: to beat the market you need information others don't have, an interpretation others haven't made, or patience others can't afford. A "strategy" is simply one of those three, organized into rules you can repeat. And from Lesson 1 you also know trading is person-against-person — so every strategy that works must answer an uncomfortable question: who is on the losing side, and why do they keep showing up?

That gives us a four-question test. Hold every strategy you ever meet — in this lesson, on YouTube, in your own head — against it:

The four questions — your strategy lie detector, forever
  1. Evidence: has it worked across decades and countries — or only in one lucky backtest window?
  2. Why does it exist: what human behavior or institutional constraint creates it? Who keeps paying, and why don't they stop?
  3. Why does it survive: what pain (drawdowns, career risk, boredom, effort) stops smart money from arbitraging it away?
  4. Can YOU run it: after Belgian TOB, spreads, taxes, and your real hours per week — does anything remain?

Notice question 3. It's the strangest and most important: a strategy with no pain attached cannot keep working — painless free money gets crowded until it's gone. The pain is not a bug. The pain is the moat.

2. The families

Six families cover almost everything real. Each card: what it is, the evidence, who's paying, the pain that guards it, and a fit score for your situation (long horizon, Belgian costs, day job).

Value

FIT: GOOD

"Buy businesses for less than they're worth; wait for the weighing machine."

What it isLesson 4, systematized: buy low-multiple stocks whose implied pessimism is exaggerated. Your P/E decoder + margin of safety, applied over and over.
EvidenceA century of data, most countries. Honest footnote: it had a brutal losing decade in the 2010s — then recovered. It breathes in decade-long waves.
Who pays youPeople overreacting to bad news, and professionals who can't buy ugly stocks — explaining a boring cement company to clients is a career risk. You have no clients.
The pain (= the moat)Value traps you must check one by one (Lesson 4), and droughts lasting years where everyone calls you an idiot. Most people break.
For youLow turnover → survives Belgian costs (Lesson 2). Uses your Lesson 3–4 skills directly. The patience requirement is real — that's the price of entry.

Momentum

FIT: PARTIAL

"What has been winning for 6–12 months tends to keep winning a while longer."

What it isBuy the strongest stocks of the past ~year, hold a month or a few, refresh. Sounds absurdly simple. It is — and it's the single most documented anomaly in finance.
EvidenceThe strongest of all: 200+ years, every asset class, every country tested — including Belgium. The academics who believe nothing believe this one.
Who pays youHuman slowness: information spreads gradually, people anchor to old prices and disbelieve trends ("it can't go higher"), then chase late. You harvest their hesitation.
The pain (= the moat)"Momentum crashes": when the market whips around (2009), momentum portfolios can lose 30–50% in weeks. And it needs constant rebalancing — turnover.
For youThe turnover is the problem: monthly rebalancing on Belgian stocks dies by TOB + spreads (Lesson 2's chart). Practical route: a cheap momentum ETF, or momentum applied only inside the liquid-US part of a portfolio. Never hand-rolled on small caps.

Quality

FIT: GOOD

"Boring excellence compounds: high returns on equity, low debt, stable margins."

What it isLesson 3's toolkit as a filter: consistently high ROE, honest cash conversion, low debt-to-equity. Buy the durable, ignore the exciting.
EvidenceRobust across markets ("profitability factor"). Quality + a sane price (quality-value blend) is one of the best-documented long-horizon combinations.
Who pays youExcitement-seekers. Lottery-ticket stocks are systematically overpriced because gamblers love them; sturdy compounders are underpriced because nobody brags about owning them.
The pain (= the moat)Mild but real: quality often looks expensive (fair P/E for a great business feels high — Lesson 4), and it lags violently fun markets. Boredom is the cost.
For youNear-perfect: very low turnover, uses your statement-reading skills, sleeps well at Belgian tax rates. The natural tilt for your long-horizon core.

Dividend / income

FIT: POOR (in Belgium)

"Own steady payers, live off the stream" — beloved, and mostly a tax mistake here.

What it isSelecting stocks for high, stable dividends. Psychologically lovely: money arrives.
EvidenceMostly a rougher version of value+quality — the dividend itself adds little that those factors don't already capture.
The Belgian problemLesson 2's math: 30% withholding domestically, the 15%+30% chain on US payers — roughly a third to 40% of the stream is gone before it reaches you. The same return taken as price growth inside an accumulating ETF compounds untaxed for decades (verify current rules — the 2026 changes matter here).
For youIf you like the companies, own them for value/quality reasons. Chasing the dividend itself, from Belgium, is voluntarily paying an extra tax. Skip — with the one exception of genuinely needing cash income.

Events & special situations

FIT: YOUR NICHE

"Profit from situations where someone must trade and cannot care about the price."

What it isSpin-offs (funds dump shares of the small company they were given), forced index-change selling, insider buying clusters, and under-covered small caps priced lazily because nobody looks.
EvidenceGood, and — crucially — structural: these edges come from rules and size limits, not psychology, so they decay slower.
Who pays youInstitutions forced by mandate: the index fund MUST sell on rebalance day; the big fund CANNOT hold the €80M spin-off. They pay for speed and size with price. You have neither constraint.
The pain (= the moat)Effort. Reading filings (Lesson 3), one situation at a time, often in Dutch or French, in stocks with scary spreads (Lesson 2: limit orders, small sizes). No shortcut exists — which is exactly why it still works.
For youThis is Book B from our master plan: Belgian small caps that no analyst covers, read via NBB filings — later with your AI agents doing the heavy reading. The one place a solo Belgian has a real structural advantage.

Passive + rebalancing

FIT: THE CORE

"Own everything cheaply, rebalance on a calendar, let compounding work."

What it isBroad accumulating ETFs + a fixed allocation (stocks/bonds/cash from your Risk Constitution), rebalanced once or twice a year — which quietly forces you to buy low and sell high.
EvidenceOverwhelming: after costs, it beats the large majority of professionals over 10+ years. It is the benchmark for a reason.
Who pays youNobody — that's the point. You're not extracting edge; you're collecting the market's built-in reward for owning productive businesses and enduring the tide.
The pain (= the moat)Only psychological: it's boring, and you must hold through −30% seasons doing nothing. Most people can't. You have a signed constitution.
For youThe foundation everything else sits on. Money not powering a deliberate, tested edge lives here (Lesson 1's "honest default") — and it's the benchmark your system must beat to justify existing.

4. Every family has a nightmare — know yours before it arrives

The idea in one sentenceAll real strategies spend years looking broken — the drought is the admission price, and quitting during it is how most people pay the price without collecting the reward.
FamilyIts historical nightmareWhat it felt like
Value~2010–2020A full decade behind the market while tech soared. Famous value investors retired, mocked. Then it turned.
Momentum2009 (and echoes)−50%+ in weeks as the market whipsawed at the crisis bottom. Years of gains erased in a season.
Qualityevery mania (1999, 2021)Watching profitless "story stocks" triple while your sturdy compounders crawl. Feeling like a fool for years.
Events / small capsevery liquidity panicSpreads blow out, tiny stocks fall hardest, nothing can be sold at sane prices. Paper losses look apocalyptic.
Passive2000–2009The "lost decade": ten years of world stocks going roughly nowhere. Patience tested to destruction.
Beginner trap #1 — strategy hopping (the invisible portfolio killer)

Watch the death spiral: you pick value. Three years of drought — you switch to momentum, right before a momentum crash. Burned, you switch to dividend stocks... Each switch happens after the old strategy's worst stretch (you sell its bottom) and into the new strategy's best-looking recent past (you buy its top). You systematically collect every family's nightmare and none of its recovery.

The fix is brutal and simple: pick strategies whose nightmare you have already imagined surviving — in euros, per your Risk Constitution — and then give them a minimum of 5 years or a written reason. The strategy statement you'll write in the homework exists precisely for the 2 a.m. moment when you want to hop.

Beginner trap #2 — choosing a strategy from its best chart

Every strategy pitch shows you its golden window: tech momentum 2019–2021 (+40%/yr!), dividend stocks 2000–2009, AI stocks since 2023. The four questions expose it: run question 1 (does it hold across decades?) and question 4 (after YOUR costs?) and the golden chart usually dies on the spot. A strategy is what survives its worst window, not what dazzled in its best.

Corollary — complexity worship: the pitch with 14 indicators and a secret sauce is not more sophisticated, it's more overfitted (your Lesson 3-in-Part-2 coin-flip demonstration will make this mathematical). Every real family above fits in one sentence. Simple and endured beats clever and abandoned, every decade.

6. Check yourself

Momentum has the best evidence of any strategy. Why is it still probably wrong for you to run by hand on Belgian stocks?

Question 4 of the lie detector: implementation. Momentum needs monthly rebalancing — high turnover — and Lesson 2 showed Belgian round trips cost ~3%+ on small caps (TOB + spreads). Ten rebalances a year eats more than the strategy earns. Evidence answers "does it work?"; costs answer "does it work for YOU?". Different questions.

Your value strategy is 3 years behind the market. When is quitting correct, and when is it the classic mistake?

Quitting is correct only if something changed in the REASON: the edge's source disappeared, or your implementation was flawed. Quitting because of the drought itself is the classic mistake — droughts are the moat that keeps the edge alive, and hopping means selling each strategy at its bottom. That's why you write the strategy statement (and its abandonment conditions) before starting, exactly like the Risk Constitution.

Why does a strategy's return typically fall 30–50% after the academic paper about it is published?

Crowding: once known, money floods in, buying the same stocks earlier and bidding away the mispricing. What survives is protected by pain (crashes, droughts), structure (too small for funds), or effort (nobody wants to read Dutch filings). Painless, easy, liquid edges die first — remember this every time an influencer offers you one.

A Belgian friend wants to build a dividend portfolio of US stocks yielding 4%. What does the tax math say?

Lesson 2's chain: 15% US withholding, then 30% Belgian tax → 4% gross becomes ≈2.4% net, every year, forever. The same companies held via an accumulating ETF compound that cash internally without the annual tax haircut. If he likes the businesses, own them for value/quality reasons — chasing the dividend itself from Belgium is a voluntary extra tax.

Why can a solo investor have a real edge in small Belgian stocks when hedge funds exist?

Structure, not intelligence: a fund managing billions cannot buy a meaningful stake in an €80M company (it would move the price for months — Lesson 1), so no analyst covers it and lazy prices persist. Add the language barrier (NBB filings in Dutch/French) and the effort moat. You're not out-thinking Goldman; you're fishing where their boat physically doesn't fit. That's Book B.

A YouTuber shows a strategy returning 40%/yr from 2019–2021. Apply the four questions out loud.

(1) Evidence: three years, one regime — the golden-window trap; test across decades or it's noise. (2) Why: no story about who pays and why. (3) Survival: if it's real and painless, it's already crowded and gone. (4) You: after TOB, spreads, and taxes? Also — Lesson 3 of Part 2 will show 1,000 coin-flippers produce many 3-year geniuses by pure luck. Verdict: entertainment, not evidence.

7. Key words from this lesson

Factor / anomaly — a documented, repeatable pattern of excess returns (value, momentum, quality…).
Value — buying implied pessimism that's exaggerated; the decoder + margin of safety, repeated.
Momentum — recent 6–12 month winners keep winning a while; strongest evidence, highest turnover.
Quality — durable profitability at a sane price; boring excellence, low turnover.
Event-driven — profiting when someone must trade regardless of price (spin-offs, index changes).
Alpha decay — edges shrinking after publication and crowding; ~⅓–½ on average.
Crowding — too much money chasing one edge until the mispricing (and then some) is gone.
Drought — a strategy's multi-year losing stretch; the admission price and the moat.
Strategy hopping — switching families after each drought; collecting every nightmare, no recoveries.
Turnover — how much you trade; the multiplier on every Belgian cost (Lesson 2).
Core–satellite — passive foundation + small deliberate tilts/niches around it.
Accumulating ETF — reinvests dividends internally; the tax-sane default for Belgians (verify current rules).

8. If you remember only three things

  1. Every real strategy names who pays and what pain guards it. No loser + no pain = no edge — just a backtest ghost or a sales pitch.
  2. The drought is the price of admission. Pick what you can hold through its historical nightmare (in euros), commit in writing, and never switch inside the pain.
  3. In Belgium, costs and taxes choose with you: low-turnover core (passive + quality/value tilt), momentum only as a cheap ETF, dividends mostly skipped — and the effort-guarded events niche as your Book B, where a solo player structurally wins.

9. Homework — write your strategy statement (~1 hr)

Field exercise

  • Run the lie detector twice: find one strategy claim in the wild (YouTube, Reddit, an ETF factsheet — "momentum", "dividend aristocrats", anything). Write its four-question answers in four sentences. Do it for a second, different family. This skill — not any single strategy — is the lesson.
  • Classify your own stocks: your two companies from Lesson 1 — which family would own each, and why? (One is probably quality-ish; check the L3 numbers you'd need to confirm it.)
  • Use the mix tool honestly, then write your Strategy Statement — one paragraph, added below your Risk Constitution in risk-constitution.md: your core, your tilt(s), your niche if any, and — most important — the conditions under which you're allowed to change it (minimum years + what evidence). This is the anti-hopping vaccine, written while calm.
  • Bonus (15 min): find one accumulating world ETF available to Belgians (e.g. on justETF): note its ticker, total cost (TER), and fund size. This is your benchmark made concrete — the thing your whole future system has to beat.
