Reading a Company: Financial Statements Without Pain

Lesson 1 taught you that in the long run, profits pull the price like gravity. This lesson teaches you where those profit numbers come from: the three documents every company must publish, how to read them in fifteen minutes, and how to spot the companies that are lying.

reading: ~45 minhomework: ~1.5 hrsprerequisite: Lessons 1–2interactive: yes — one detective game

1. Three documents, three questions

The idea in one sentenceEverything about a company's money fits in three documents, and each one answers exactly one question.

Think about your own money for a moment. If a friend asked "how are you doing financially?", a complete answer has three parts:

  • "How much did you earn and spend this year?" — your salary and your expenses.
  • "What do you own and owe right now?" — your apartment, your savings, your car loan.
  • "What actually moved through your bank account?" — the real cash in and out.

Companies answer the same three questions with three official documents, published every year (and in shorter form every quarter):

To learn them, we'll use one company through the whole lesson: Sint-Anna Brewing — our fictional Belgian brewery from the order book, now grown up. It sells €10 million of beer per year. Real company statements have more lines, but the skeleton is always exactly this.

2. The income statement — the story of €100 of beer

The idea in one sentenceThe income statement starts with everything the company sold, then subtracts costs layer by layer, until what's left is profit.

Read every income statement top to bottom, as money flowing downhill. The easiest way: follow what happens to €100 of sales:

Sint-Anna Brewing — €100 of beer sold, step by step
  1. Revenue (also called "sales" or "turnover"): customers paid €100 for beer. This is the top line — before ANY costs.
  2. Making the beer costs money — barley, hops, bottles, the brewery workers. This is cost of goods sold: −€40. What remains, €60, is gross profit.
  3. Running the company also costs money — office salaries, marketing, trucks, accountants: −€40. What remains, €20, is operating profit: what the business itself earns.
  4. The bank wants interest on the company's loans: −€3.
  5. The government wants tax: −€4.50.
  6. What survives is net profit: €12.50. This is "the profit" from Lesson 1 — the number behind EPS and P/E.
Beginner trap #1 — comparing margins across different businesses

A supermarket with a 2% net margin can be excellent — supermarkets sell enormous volumes with tiny margins. A software company with a 15% margin might be weak — its rivals make 30%. Margins only mean something compared to similar companies, and compared to the same company's own past. A number alone is never good or bad.

3. The balance sheet — the photo of what it owns and owes

The idea in one sentenceEverything a company owns was paid for with either borrowed money or the owners' money — so owns = owed + owners', always, to the cent.

Start with something you know. Your friend "owns" a €300,000 apartment — but €200,000 of mortgage is still unpaid. What is truly hers? €300,000 − €200,000 = €100,000. Three words describe this:

  • Assets — everything owned: the apartment (€300,000).
  • Liabilities — everything owed: the mortgage (€200,000).
  • Equity — what's truly yours after debts: €100,000.

A balance sheet is exactly this, for a company, on one specific day. Here is Sint-Anna Brewing's — drawn as two towers that are always the same height:

Two quick health checks you can compute from any balance sheet:

Balance sheet health checks — Sint-Anna's numbers
  1. Debt-to-equity: borrowed money ÷ owners' money = €5.0M ÷ €6.0M ≈ 0.8. Below ~1 is usually comfortable; above ~2, the bank owns more of the company's future than the shareholders do. (Sector matters, as always.)
  2. ROE (return on equity): net profit ÷ equity = €1.25M ÷ €6.0M ≈ 21%. Meaning: the owners' money grows ~21% per year inside the business. Consistently above ~15% is a good business. But careful — more debt makes ROE look better while making the company riskier. Always read ROE and debt together.

4. The cash flow statement — the lie detector

The idea in one sentenceProfit is an opinion, cash is a fact — and the cash flow statement is where the facts live.

This surprises everyone at first: a company can report a profit while its bank account goes down. How? Watch:

How profit and cash separate — two true stories from the brewery
  1. Sint-Anna sells €500,000 of beer to a hotel chain, with payment due in 90 days. Accounting rules say: the sale happened, so it counts as revenue and profit today. But the bank account received €0. Profit went up; cash didn't.
  2. Sint-Anna buys a new brewing machine for €800,000, paid in cash today. Accounting rules say: the machine will work ~10 years, so the cost is spread over 10 years on paper (€80,000/year — this spreading is called depreciation). Profit barely moved this year; the bank account lost €800,000 at once.

Neither of these is cheating — the rules are sensible. But they create a gap between "profit" (a calculated opinion) and "cash" (a bank fact). Honest companies show both. Dishonest companies show beautiful profits while cash quietly bleeds — and the cash flow statement is where you catch them. It has three parts:

Beginner trap #2 — believing the profit number without the cash check

Wirecard, a German payment company worth €24 billion, reported beautiful growing profits for years. In 2020 it admitted that €1.9 billion of its cash did not exist. The profits were paper. People who read only income statements lost everything; people who noticed that reported profits never matched the cash flows had asked the right question years earlier.

Your rule, forever: never judge a company by its profit without looking at its operating cash flow. Profit is an opinion. Cash is a fact.

6. The beginner's toolkit — five numbers and where to find them

The idea in one sentenceFive numbers, always judged against the sector and the company's own history, answer "is this a good business?" faster than any news article.

*Rough guides for a first filter — every sector has its own normal ranges. Lesson 4 (valuation) builds on these five.

Where the documents live:

  • US companies: every listed company must file a yearly 10-K (the full annual report) and quarterly 10-Qs on sec.gov/edgar — free, complete, searchable. The three statements are in the section called "Financial Statements".
  • Belgian companies: the annual report is on the company's own "Investor Relations" page. Additionally — and almost nobody uses this — every Belgian company, listed or not, must file annual accounts at the NBB Balance Sheet Centre (Balanscentrale / Centrale des bilans), publicly accessible. This is the under-fished data source from our master plan, and your future agents will read it automatically.
Beginner trap #3 — the "record profit" that happened only once

A company sells its headquarters building and books a €50M gain. This year's net profit doubles! The headline says "record profit". But the company can't sell its headquarters again next year — this is a one-time gain, not the business improving.

Check: annual reports separate "operating" results from one-time items. When profit jumps suddenly, always ask: did the business improve, or did something unrepeatable happen? Operating profit, not net profit, is the line that tells you how the actual business is doing.

7. Check yourself

Answer first, then click. All six are patterns you will meet in real annual reports.

A company reports rising profit for 3 years, but its operating cash flow is negative all 3 years. What does this suggest?

The profits may exist only on paper: customers who haven't paid, aggressive accounting, costs pushed forward. Real, healthy profit turns into cash within a reasonable time. Persistent profit-without-cash is the single loudest warning in accounting — it was visible for years at Wirecard and at our fictional TechnoBel.

A company owns €300M of assets and owes €200M. What is its equity, and what exactly do you buy when you buy its shares?

Equity = €300M − €200M = €100M. Buying a share buys a piece of that €100M — not of the €300M. Lenders get paid first, always. That's why two companies with identical assets can be completely different investments if one carries triple the debt.

The brewery sells €500,000 of beer to a hotel chain, payment due in 90 days. What happens to profit today? What happens to cash today?

Profit goes up today (the sale is recorded when it's made). Cash: nothing — €0 arrived. This is the fundamental gap between the income statement (opinions and rules) and the cash flow statement (bank facts). Neither is wrong; you must read both.

A supermarket has a 2% net margin. A software company has 15%. Which is the weaker business?

You cannot tell — margins are only meaningful within a sector. 2% can be excellent for supermarkets (huge volume, thin margins by nature); 15% could be poor for software if competitors earn 30%. Compare to similar companies and to the same company's own history, never across sectors.

Company X has ROE of 25%. Company Y has ROE of 15%. X carries debt-to-equity of 3.0; Y carries 0.3. Which would you rather own long term?

Usually Y. Debt inflates ROE: borrowing shrinks the equity base, so the same profit divided by less equity gives a prettier percentage — while making the company fragile. X's 25% is partly a debt trick; one bad year and the bank owns the conversation. Always read ROE together with debt.

A company's net profit doubled this year. Before celebrating, what two questions do you ask?

First: was it the business, or a one-time item (sold a building, legal settlement, accounting change)? Check operating profit. Second: did cash follow — does operating cash flow confirm the jump? If both answers are good, then celebrate.

8. Key words from this lesson

Revenue — everything customers paid this period. The top line, before all costs.
Gross / operating / net profit — what survives after production costs / after running costs / after everything.
Margin — a profit level as % of revenue. Lets you compare companies of any size.
Asset — anything the company owns: cash, stock, machines, buildings.
Liability — anything the company owes: loans, unpaid bills.
Equity — assets minus liabilities: the part that truly belongs to shareholders.
Debt-to-equity — borrowed money ÷ owners' money. Above ~2 = the bank is the real boss.
ROE — net profit ÷ equity: how fast owners' money grows inside the business.
Depreciation — spreading a big purchase over its working years on paper.
Operating cash flow — real cash the business itself generated. The lie detector.
One-time gain — profit from something unrepeatable. Not the business improving.
10-K / annual report — the full yearly document; on EDGAR (US) or the company's IR page (BE).

9. If you remember only three things

  1. Three documents, three questions. Income statement: did it earn? Balance sheet: what does it own and owe? Cash flow: did real money move? Fifteen minutes with these beats any headline.
  2. Profit is an opinion, cash is a fact. Never trust a profit number that operating cash flow doesn't confirm, year after year.
  3. No number means anything alone. Every margin, every ratio: compare to the sector, and to the company's own last five years. Trends beat snapshots.

10. Homework — read a real annual report (the easy way)

Field exercise (~1.5 hours)

Take the Belgian company from your Lesson 1 homework (Lotus Bakeries, Melexis, Kinepolis — any of them). Go to its website → "Investors" → download the latest annual report. Don't read it cover to cover — hunt for exactly these numbers:

  • From the income statement: revenue and net profit, this year and last year. Compute: revenue growth and net margin.
  • From the balance sheet: total equity and total financial debt. Compute: debt-to-equity and ROE.
  • From the cash flow statement: operating cash flow. Compute: cash conversion = operating CF ÷ net profit.
  • Then write three sentences, one per statement: Does it earn money? Is it safely financed? Does the profit convert to real cash?

Bonus (10 min): open sec.gov/edgar, search "Microsoft", open the latest 10-K, and just find the income statement inside it. You don't need to read it — you need to lose your fear of the document. It's the same three statements, just longer.

Bring your five computed numbers and your three sentences. That's a real company analysis — your first.
