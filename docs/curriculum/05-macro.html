Macro: Rates, Inflation, and Why Everything Moves Together

Lesson 4 ended with a mystery: interest rates rose, and every stock on Earth fell — the good ones too. This lesson explains the tide that lifts and drops all boats: central banks, inflation, bonds, and the economic seasons. And it ends with the honest answer to "so how do I profit from macro?" — which is not what the internet tells you.

1. Your stock is a boat. Macro is the tide.

The idea in one sentenceEven a perfectly chosen stock swims in a tide that moves all stocks together — and that tide has exactly two engines: interest rates and the economic cycle.

Remember the horizon chart from Lesson 1: a big part of every stock's movement is not about the company at all. When the tide comes in, bad companies rise too. When it goes out, great companies sink too. The beta number you saw in the quote table measures exactly this: how strongly a stock follows the tide.

Macro ("macroeconomics") is the study of that tide. You need enough of it to never be confused by your portfolio again — not to predict the future. Two engines drive nearly everything: the price of money (this section and the next) and the economic seasons (section 5).

2. Central banks — the thermostat of the economy

The idea in one sentenceA central bank sets the price of borrowing money, and it moves that price up or down to keep inflation near 2% — like a thermostat.

Every currency has a central bank: the ECB for the euro (Frankfurt), the Fed for the dollar (Washington). They are not normal banks — they are the banks of the banks, and they set one crucial number: the policy rate — the interest rate at which banks themselves borrow. That one number ripples outward into everything: your mortgage, a company's loan, government bond yields, and — through Lesson 4's formula — the value of every stock.

3. Inflation — the silent tax on standing still

The idea in one sentenceInflation quietly shrinks what your money can buy — slowly enough that you don't feel it, fast enough to halve your savings in a generation.
What 3% inflation does to €10,000 hidden under the bed
  1. Each year, prices rise 3%. So each year your money buys 1 ÷ 1.03 ≈ 2.9% less.
  2. After 10 years: €10,000 ÷ 1.03¹⁰ = €7,441 of today's buying power.
  3. After 20 years: €5,537. After 30 years: €4,120.

4. Bonds — the seesaw that rules the world

The idea in one sentenceA bond is a tradable loan, and its price moves opposite to interest rates — a seesaw that explains half of all financial news.

A bond is simple: you lend money (to a government or company), they pay you fixed interest (the coupon) every year, and return the full amount at the end. Bonds trade on markets just like shares. The bond market is even bigger than the stock market, and it sets the "safe rate" that anchors Lesson 4's discount rate. One mechanism to understand deeply:

The seesaw — why rates up means bond prices down
  1. Last year you bought a government bond: €1,000, paying 2% (€20/year) for 10 years.
  2. Today the central bank has raised rates, and new bonds pay 4% (€40/year).
  3. You want to sell yours. Who would pay €1,000 for €20/year when €1,000 now buys €40/year? Nobody.
  4. To find a buyer, your price must drop until your €20/year becomes competitive — for a 10-year bond, to roughly €840. You lost ~16% on a "safe" investment, purely because rates rose.

Flip it around: when rates fall, old bonds with fat coupons become precious and their prices rise. Rates up → bond prices down. Rates down → bond prices up. Always. And the longer the bond has left to run, the harder the seesaw swings it — exactly the "far money reacts hardest" rule from Lesson 4, because a bond IS pure far money.

One more bond fact you'll meet in every market summary: the yield curve — the line of interest rates from short loans (3 months) to long ones (10+ years). Normally long rates are higher (more waiting, more risk). When short rates rise above long rates (an "inverted" curve), it historically often signaled a recession within a year or two — the market betting that the central bank has braked too hard. Useful context; imperfect prophet.

6. The economic seasons — and why the market moves first

The idea in one sentenceThe economy breathes in cycles of a few years — and the stock market moves 6–12 months ahead of it, so trading on today's economic news is trading the past.

Economies don't grow in straight lines. They breathe: expansion (growth, hiring, optimism) → peak (overheating, inflation, rate hikes) → recession (shrinking, layoffs, fear) → recovery (rate cuts, cheap money, rebirth). Beginner trap #1 — trading the announcement

CPI (the monthly inflation number) comes out at 14:30. Your plan: read it fast, trade fast, profit. Reality: algorithms react in microseconds (Lesson 1 — you know who's on the other side now), the first minutes are whipsaw chaos, spreads widen (Lesson 2), and the "obvious" direction reverses half the time because what matters is the number versus expectations, and expectations were already priced.

Long-horizon rule: know the calendar (CPI days, central bank meetings) — not to trade them, but to avoid placing orders around them. Let the dust settle; your horizon is months, not minutes.

Beginner trap #2 — "recession is coming, I'll sell everything and buy back at the bottom"

Three problems. One: economists and headlines have predicted roughly nine of the last five recessions — the signal is unreliable. Two: the market leads — by the time the recession is visible, the fall largely happened. Three: re-entry. The market bottoms at maximum fear; the news at the bottom is always apocalyptic; almost nobody who sold gets back in near it. They wait for "clarity", which arrives 40% higher.

The recovery's biggest days cluster right around the bottom — miss a handful of them and you miss a huge part of the decade's return. Timing the cycle with your whole portfolio is how patient money becomes impatient losses.

7. So what do you actually DO with macro?

The idea in one sentenceMacro is for positioning and protection, not prediction — it tells you what weather is possible, not tomorrow's forecast.

The honest truth that separates professionals from storytellers: nobody reliably predicts macro. Not banks, not hedge funds, not the famous person on YouTube with the confident voice. The few who nail one call (a crash, an inflation wave) usually miss the next one loudly. So what's macro for? Four real uses:

  • Never be confused. When rates jump and your best stock falls 15% on no news, you now know: gravity changed, not your analysis (Lesson 4). Confusion causes panic-selling; understanding prevents it.
  • Stress-test your portfolio, don't forecast. Ask "IF rates rise 2%, what happens to what I own?" — if the answer is "everything I own dies together" (all growth stocks, all long bonds), fix the portfolio now, whatever your forecast.
  • Diversify across weather. Own things that suffer in different seasons: some defensive, some cyclical, some short-duration, some long. Not because you know the season — because you don't.
  • Respect the calendar. Know when CPI and central-bank days are; don't place Belgian limit orders into that chaos.

This maps exactly onto our future system (master plan, the debate we ran): macro enters as risk scaling — "how exposed are we to each weather?" — never as a prediction signal. Regime awareness, not regime forecasting. Even the professionals who tried to model regime prediction mostly overfit it; we decided from day one not to play that game.

8. Check yourself

The ECB raises rates by 1%. Walk the chain: why does your favorite growth stock fall 20% "on no news about the company"?

Policy rate up → safe rates up → everyone's discount rate up → Lesson 4's formula: value = profit ÷ (rate − growth). For a growth stock the (rate−growth) gap is tiny, so adding 1% to the rate shrinks the value violently (e.g. 1/0.03 → 1/0.04 = −25%). The company changed nothing; the gravity changed.

You own a 10-year bond paying 2%. New bonds pay 4%. Why is yours suddenly worth ~16% less?

Nobody will pay full price for €20/year when the same money now buys €40/year. Your bond's price must fall until its fixed coupon matches the new reality. The seesaw: rates up, existing bond prices down — and the longer the bond, the bigger the swing.

The news announces: "the recession has officially begun." Stocks rise 2% that day. Explain without magic.

The market smelled the recession a year ago and already fell then — prices are expectations (Lesson 1). By announcement day, the recession is old news, fully priced. If the official news is even slightly less bad than feared, the surprise is positive and prices rise. The market trades the future; the news reports the past.

Your savings account pays 1%. Inflation is 3%. What is really happening to your money, and how bad is it over 20 years?

Real return = 1% − 3% = −2% per year: a guaranteed slow loss. Over 20 years: ×(0.98)²⁰ ≈ −33% of buying power. "Safe" cash is only safe against volatility — not against inflation. That's the deepest reason to own productive assets at all.

US CPI comes out in 10 minutes. You were about to place a limit order on a Belgian small-cap. Wait or send?

Wait. Around macro announcements spreads widen, liquidity thins, and prices whipsaw — even on stocks far from the news. Your horizon is months; ten minutes of patience costs nothing and avoids being the counterparty who provides someone's lunch during the chaos.

The Fed hikes while the ECB holds. What tends to happen to the dollar — and name BOTH forces acting on your US stocks (in euros).

Higher US rates attract money seeking interest → the dollar tends to strengthen against the euro. Your US holdings then face two opposite forces: the hike pressures their valuations down (gravity), while the stronger dollar makes each dollar worth more in euros. Sometimes they cancel, sometimes they don't — this is Lesson 2's double bet, now with its engine visible.

9. Key words from this lesson

Central bank — the bank of the banks (ECB, Fed); sets the price of money.
Policy rate — the base interest rate that ripples into all loans, bonds, and valuations.
Inflation / CPI — the speed of price rises / the monthly index that measures it.
Real return — your return minus inflation; the only return that buys anything.
Bond / coupon / yield — a tradable loan / its fixed interest / its effective return at today's price.
The seesaw — rates up ⇒ existing bond prices down, and vice versa. Longer bond, bigger swing.
Yield curve — rates from short to long maturities; inversion has often preceded recessions.
Expansion / recession — the economy's in-breath and out-breath; a full cycle ≈ 4–10 years.
Defensive / cyclical — bought-in-any-weather sectors vs. postponable-purchase sectors.
The market leads — stocks turn 6–12 months before the economy; news trades the past.
Stress test — asking "what if rates/growth surprise?" about your portfolio, instead of forecasting.
Regime — the current macro weather; our system will measure exposure to it, never predict it.

10. If you remember only three things

  1. Rates are the gravity of everything. One policy rate flows into bonds (seesaw), stocks (the Lesson 4 formula), currencies, and your mortgage. When everything moves together, look at rates first.
  2. The market moves before the economy. Selling on recession headlines and buying on all-clear headlines is systematically backwards — that's trading the past at the prices of the future.
  3. Macro is armor, not a crystal ball. Use it to understand, stress-test and diversify. The moment you're sure what macro does next, re-read this lesson.
