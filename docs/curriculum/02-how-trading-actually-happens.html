2. The three order types you must know

The idea in one sentenceA market order buys now at an unknown price; a limit order buys at a known price but maybe never; a stop order is a tripwire that fires a market order when a level is touched.

When you send an order, you must choose its type. The type answers one question: what matters more to you — speed, or price?

Order typeWhat you're sayingYou controlYou risk
Market"Buy/sell NOW, at whatever price is available."Speed — you fill instantly.Price — you eat spread + slippage (Lesson 1's €45).
Limit"Buy only at €20.05 or better. Otherwise, wait."Price — never worse than your number.Fill — maybe you never buy at all.
Stop"IF the price touches €19.00, THEN sell me at market."A trigger level.Both — it fires a market order, at whatever price comes next.

Words don't teach this — the order book does. Same book as Lesson 1, but now you have three different buttons. Try all three (reset between tries) and watch how differently they behave:

What you should have seen, in words:

  • Market BUY 600 — everything fills instantly, but at an average of €20.075, and the price jumps. Fast and expensive. (Exactly Lesson 1.)
  • Limit BUY 600 @ €20.05 — 300 fill immediately at €20.05 (there was a seller there). The other 300 become a green bar in the book: you are now the best buyer, waiting at €20.05. Maybe a seller comes down to you. Maybe not. Price certain, fill uncertain.
  • Limit BUY 600 @ €19.95 — nothing fills. Your price is below every seller. Your order joins the line at €19.95 — behind the 400 shares already waiting there (first come, first served). The patient version: you only buy if the price comes down to you.
In simple wordsA market order takes from the book and pays the spread. A limit order joins the book and waits. On big liquid stocks the difference is cents. On small Belgian stocks the difference is your profit.

The stop order — a tripwire, not a guarantee

A stop order (often "stop-loss") protects a position you already own. You own shares at €20 and tell the broker: "if the price touches €19.00, sell everything." It sounds like insurance. Understand precisely what it does:

A stop-loss in slow motion — the good case and the ugly case
  1. You own 500 shares, bought at €20.00. You set a stop at €19.00 ("max loss ~5%").
  2. Good case: the price drifts down calmly, touches €19.00 → your stop triggers a market order → fills near €18.98. Loss ≈ 5%. It worked.
  3. Ugly case: bad news comes overnight. The stock closed at €19.80. It opens tomorrow at €16.00 — no trading happened between: prices can jump, they don't slide through every number.
  4. Your stop triggers at the first price at or below €19.00 — which is €16.00. You sell at €16.00, not €19.00. Loss: 20%, not 5%.

So: a stop order limits losses in calm, continuous falls. It cannot protect you from overnight gaps — nothing can, except position size (Lesson 6). There is also a stop-limit variant ("if €19.00 touches, place a limit at €18.80") — it protects the price but risks not selling at all while the stock keeps falling. Every choice trades one risk for another.

Beginner trap #1 — the stop-loss placed inside the noise

Lesson 1 showed that daily moves are mostly random noise. A typical stock wanders ±2% in a normal week, meaning: a stop 3% below your buy price will almost certainly be hit by pure randomness — you get sold out, pay the costs, and then watch the stock recover without you.

If you use stops at all: place them below the level where your reason for owning the stock would be wrong — not at "a round number close by so I feel safe". Feeling safe is expensive.

One more setting on every order ticket: time in force. "DAY" = the order dies tonight if not filled. "GTC" (good-till-cancelled) = it stays alive for weeks until filled or cancelled. GTC sounds convenient. It has a hidden danger — trap #2:

Beginner trap #2 — the forgotten GTC order fills at the worst moment

You place a GTC limit: "buy at €18.00" (price now €20.00). Three weeks later you've forgotten it. Ask yourself: what has to happen for the price to fall to €18? Usually: bad news.

So your old order fills exactly when informed people are selling — you buy at €18 because something is wrong, and by then your reason for wanting the stock may be dead. Professionals call this adverse selection: a resting order is a free gift to better-informed traders. Rule: check your open orders weekly, and prefer DAY orders you consciously renew.

4. Auctions — how Euronext really opens, closes, and trades small caps

The idea in one sentenceAn auction collects everyone's orders first, then computes the single price where the most shares can trade — and everyone trades at that one price.

Not all trading is continuous. At certain moments the exchange pauses matching, collects orders in a pile, and then finds one fair price. This is an auction. It works like this:

An auction in 4 steps
  1. For some minutes, orders arrive but nothing trades. The pile grows: buyers with their limits, sellers with theirs.
  2. The exchange computer tries every possible price and asks: "at this price, how many shares can actually trade?"
  3. It picks the price where the most shares match. Example: at €20.02, buyers want 5,000 and sellers offer 5,200 → 5,000 trade.
  4. Everyone who matches trades at that single price — €20.02 for all, regardless of what limit they wrote (if it was €20.02 or better).

Why you must know this, trading Belgium:

  • Every Euronext day opens with an auction (~09:00) and closes with one (~17:30–17:35). The closing auction is the most liquid moment of the whole day — big funds trade there. For mid-size Belgian names, the closing auction is often the best place for your limit order.
  • Many small Belgian stocks don't trade continuously at all. They trade by double fixing: two auctions per day (around 11:30 and 16:30), and nothing in between. If you send a market order to such a stock, it waits for the next auction and takes whatever price comes out. Check your stock's "trading mode" on euronext.com before your first order — homework includes this.

Two of these deserve slow math. First, what actually reaches you from a US dividend:

A $100 US dividend, step by step (Belgian investor)
  1. The company pays $100 gross dividend for your shares.
  2. The US takes 15% first (thanks to the tax treaty — but only if your broker filed your W-8BEN form; without it, 30%): $100 − 15% = $85.
  3. Belgium then takes 30% of what remains: $85 × 30% = $25.50.
  4. You keep: $85 − $25.50 = $59.50. Almost 40% of the dividend is gone before it touches you.

A Belgian dividend is simpler: €100 − 30% = €70. Lesson for later: dividend-heavy strategies must be judged on net dividends, not the pretty gross yield on the website.

6. Buying American stocks with euros — the double bet

The idea in one sentenceWhen you buy a US stock, you always make two bets at once: on the stock, and on the dollar.
The hidden second bet, step by step
  1. You convert €10,000 → $10,800 (at 1.08) and buy Microsoft.
  2. One year later Microsoft is up 10%: your shares are worth $11,880.
  3. But the dollar weakened 10% against the euro over that year (this happens — 2025 was worse than that).
  4. Converting back: your $11,880 becomes ≈ €10,000. The stock won 10%; the currency took it all back. You made nothing.

The reverse also happens — the dollar can add to your gains. The point is not to fear it, but to know it: a US portfolio is always also a dollar position. Over many years currency swings partly cancel out; over one year they can dominate your result. (Your future system will measure this separately — that's part of "attribution" from the master plan.)

7. Check yourself

Answer first, then click. Each question is one of this lesson's core ideas in disguise.

Why should you basically never send a market order for a Belgian small-cap?

Thin book + wide spread. Your market order eats through the few cheap sellers and fills at bad prices (the simulator's €45 extra, but worse). With a limit order you set the worst price you accept — you risk not filling, but never overpaying. On small caps, "not filling" is a much cheaper problem than "filling badly".

You own a stock at €20 with a stop-loss at €19. Terrible news comes overnight, and the stock opens at €16. At what price do you sell?

About €16. A stop is a tripwire: when the price touches or jumps past €19, it fires a market order at the next available price — €16. Stops protect against slow slides, never against gaps. Only position size protects against gaps.

What is dangerous about a "good-till-cancelled" buy limit far below the current price?

It usually fills exactly when something bad happened — that's what pushed the price down to your level. You buy from better-informed sellers at the worst moment, and your original reason for buying may no longer be true. Old resting orders are gifts to informed traders. Review open orders weekly.

Your friend trades his whole Belgian portfolio about once a month. The market averages ~7%/year. What return does his skill need to produce per year just to break even?

About 38% of costs (12 × 3.2%) minus the 7% the market gives ≈ he needs to beat the market by roughly 31 percentage points every year just to reach zero. The best investors in history beat the market by 10–20 points. His system is mathematically doomed by frequency alone, before we know anything about his skill.

A US stock pays you a $100 dividend. Your broker filed the W-8BEN. How much lands in your pocket as a Belgian investor?

$59.50. The US takes 15% ($15), leaving $85. Belgium takes 30% of the $85 ($25.50). Net: $59.50 — about 40% is gone. Always judge dividend strategies on net numbers.

You want to buy a tiny Belgian stock and notice it only trades at 11:30 and 16:30. What is happening, and what should you do?

It's a "double fixing" stock — it trades only in two auctions per day, no continuous trading. Place a limit order and let it participate in the auction: the exchange finds the single price where the most shares match, and everyone trades at that price. Never send a market order into an auction on a thin stock — you accept whatever price falls out.

8. Key words from this lesson

Fill — your order (or part of it) got executed at some price.
Settlement — the official ownership transfer: T+1 (US) or T+2 (Europe) days after the trade.
Market order — "fill me now at any price." Controls speed, risks price.
Limit order — "only at my price or better." Controls price, risks never filling.
Stop order — a tripwire: when the level is touched, it fires a market order.
Stop-limit — tripwire that fires a limit order: protects price, risks not selling at all.
DAY / GTC — order dies tonight / order stays alive until cancelled.
Adverse selection — old resting orders fill exactly when informed people trade against you.
Auction — orders collect, then one price where the most shares match; all trade at it.
Double fixing — small Euronext stocks that trade only in two auctions per day.
TOB — Belgian tax on every buy and every sell (~0.35% for shares).
W-8BEN — the form that cuts US dividend tax from 30% to 15% for you.

9. If you remember only three things

  1. Limit orders, almost always. Market orders are acceptable only for small amounts on giant liquid stocks. Everywhere else, you set the price or you don't trade.
  2. Frequency is the silent killer. The market gives ~7% a year; trading a Belgian portfolio monthly costs ~38% a year. Nothing you learn later matters if this one number is against you.
  3. A stop order is a tripwire, not insurance. It works in slow declines and fails in gaps. Real protection is position size — coming in Lesson 6.

10. Homework — place real (paper) orders

Field exercise (~2 hours, zero risk — paper money)

Step 1 — open the account (the important step). Create an Interactive Brokers account and activate paper trading mode (simulated money, real market data). This account becomes our laboratory for the whole curriculum. Any broker demo works if IBKR is too much paperwork this week.

Step 2 — run these four experiments during market hours, and write down every fill price next to the bid/ask you saw at that moment:

  • Market BUY 5 shares of a US giant (e.g. Microsoft). Note: fill price vs. the ask you saw. How much did you lose to the spread?
  • Limit BUY the same stock 1% below the current price, DAY order. Did it fill by the end of the day?
  • Limit BUY a Belgian stock (e.g. Kinepolis) at the current bid. How long until it fills — if at all? First: check on euronext.com whether it trades continuously or by auction, and note the auction times.
  • Set a stop-sell 5% below your Microsoft position. Then look at the stock's recent daily moves and answer: is this stop inside the random noise?

Step 3 — compute the full round-trip cost of your Belgian trade: spread you paid + 2 × 0.35% TOB + commission. Compare it to the US trade. This number is yours now — you measured it.

Bring the fills and the two cost numbers to the next session.
