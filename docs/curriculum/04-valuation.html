Valuation: What Is a Company Actually Worth?

In Lesson 1 you asked the bakery question: "how many years of profit am I paying?" This lesson gives that question its full machinery: how professionals put a number on a business, why that number is always a range, and how to read what the market secretly believes inside any price.

reading: ~45 minhomework: ~1 hrprerequisite: Lessons 1 & 3interactive: yes — a value machine with sliders

1. €100 today is not €100 next year

The idea in one sentenceMoney in the future is worth less than money today — and "how much less" is the key that unlocks all valuation.

Simple question: would you rather receive €100 today, or €100 in one year?

Today, obviously. If you get €100 today, you can put it in a safe government bond paying, say, 5%, and have €105 next year. So "€100 today" and "€105 next year" are the same thing. Which means: "€100 next year" is worth less than €100 today. How much exactly?

Translating future money into today's money — this is called discounting
  1. Safe interest rate: 5% per year.
  2. €100 arriving in 1 year is worth today: €100 ÷ 1.05 = €95.24. (Check it: €95.24 growing at 5% for a year = €100. Same thing.)
  3. €100 arriving in 2 years: divide twice: €100 ÷ 1.05 ÷ 1.05 = €90.70.
  4. €100 arriving in 10 years: €100 ÷ 1.05¹⁰ = €61.39. Far money is small money.

This translation is called discounting, and the interest rate used is the discount rate. One more thing before we use it: for a risky promise, you demand more than 5%. A government almost certainly pays; a brewery might not. So for a company you might demand 8% or 10% per year to accept the risk. Higher risk → higher discount rate → future profits worth less today. Hold that thought — it becomes the punchline of this lesson.

2. The value machine — a company is its discounted future profits

The idea in one sentenceA company's value = all the profits it will ever make, each one translated into today's money — and one small formula captures it.

Remember what a share is (Lesson 1): a claim on all future profits. So valuing a company means: estimate the future profits, discount each one to today, add them all up. Done by hand this is called a DCF ("discounted cash flow"). For a stable company there is a beautiful shortcut — one line:

The shortcut formula (worth memorizing — it's the whole lesson)
  1. Value = next year's profit ÷ (discount rate − growth rate)
  2. Sint-Anna Brewing earns €1.25M, growing ~3% per year. You demand 8% for the risk.
  3. Value = €1.25M ÷ (0.08 − 0.03) = €1.25M ÷ 0.05 = €25M.
  4. Sanity check with Lesson 1: value ÷ profit = €25M ÷ €1.25M = 20. That's a P/E of 20 — the formula and the bakery question are the same idea. Fair P/E = 1 ÷ (rate − growth).

3. Reading the market's mind — what a P/E secretly says

The idea in one sentenceSince fair P/E = 1 ÷ (rate − growth), every P/E you see on a screen is a growth belief in disguise — and you can decode it.

Flip the formula around. If the market demands ~8% from stocks, then each P/E level implies a growth expectation:

P/E you seeImplied belief (at 8% demanded return)Translation
P/E 8growth ≈ −4.5% / yr"This business is shrinking and will keep shrinking."
P/E 12.5growth ≈ 0%"Frozen — same profit forever."
P/E 20growth ≈ 3% / yr"Grows a little faster than the economy." (Sint-Anna)
P/E 33growth ≈ 5% / yr"A durable winner — must out-grow the economy for decades."
P/E 60growth ≈ 6.3% / yr, forever"A near-miracle. Possible — a handful per generation manage it."

This is the decoder ring for the surprise machine from Lesson 1. A stock at P/E 60 isn't "expensive" by decree — it's a promise: profits must compound near-miraculously for decades. If reality delivers merely "very good", the price falls. A stock at P/E 8 is priced for decay; if the business merely survives, the price rises. Your job as an investor is never "is the P/E high?" — it is "is the promise inside this P/E reasonable?"

Beginner trap #1 — the value trap: cheap for a reason

A retail chain trades at P/E 6. "So cheap!" But decode it: P/E 6 implies the market expects profits to shrink ~8% per year — perhaps online shopping is eating it alive. If the market is right, that "cheap" stock is correctly priced or still too expensive. It gets cheaper every year, forever. This is a value trap.

A low multiple is not a bargain — it's a diagnosis to verify. The question is always: "the market expects decline — do I have a real reason to believe the market is wrong?" (Recall Lesson 1: information, interpretation, or patience. Which one do you have here?)

4. The multiples family — five quick rulers

The idea in one sentenceMultiples are shortcut rulers: price compared to profit, assets, sales, or dividends — each useful somewhere, dangerous somewhere else.
MultipleFormulaBest forDanger
P/Eprice ÷ profit per shareStable, profitable companies. Your default ruler.Meaningless if profit is tiny, negative, or one-time-inflated (Lesson 3).
P/Bprice ÷ equity per shareBanks, insurers, asset-heavy firms — where the balance sheet IS the business.Useless for software/brands: their real assets aren't on the books.
P/Sprice ÷ sales per shareYoung companies with no profit yet.Sales without profit can be bought at any price — remember TechnoBel (L3).
Dividend yielddividend ÷ priceMature cash-payers; comparing to bond interest.A giant yield often means the market expects the dividend to be cut. Judge on NET (L2 tax math).
EV/EBITDA(market cap + debt − cash) ÷ raw operating earningsComparing firms with different debt levels; used by professionals and buyers of whole companies.EBITDA ignores real costs (machines wear out). "Earnings before the bad stuff."

How professionals actually use these: relative valuation. You never judge a multiple alone (Lesson 3's rule) — you compare it three ways:

  • Against peers: Lotus Bakeries P/E vs. other premium food companies — not vs. banks or software.
  • Against its own history: is it at the top or bottom of its usual 5-year range? A great company at its historically highest multiple has its greatness more than priced in.
  • Against the decoded promise: use the table above — is the implied growth actually believable for this business?

5. Why interest rates move everything — the gravity of valuation

The idea in one sentenceThe discount rate contains the interest rate — so when central banks raise rates, every valuation on Earth falls, and the "expensive growth" stocks fall hardest.

Look at the formula again: value = profit ÷ (rate − growth). Where does your demanded rate come from? It starts from the safe interest rate (what governments pay) plus extra for risk. When central banks raise interest rates, the safe rate rises, so everyone's discount rate rises, so all values mechanically fall — no company did anything wrong. Now watch who gets hurt most:

Rates rise from 6% to 9% — two companies, same profit of €1M
  1. Slow & steady (growth 1%): value at 6%: 1 ÷ (0.06−0.01) = €20.0M. At 9%: 1 ÷ 0.08 = €12.5M. Fall: −37%. Painful.
  2. Fast grower (growth 5%): value at 6%: 1 ÷ 0.01 = €100M. At 9%: 1 ÷ 0.04 = €25M. Fall: −75%. Catastrophic.

6. The margin of safety — how professionals stay honest

The idea in one sentenceBecause your value estimate is a range, only act when the price is far outside that range — the gap is your protection against your own errors.

You've seen that moving one slider by one point swings the value by 30–70%. So your honest output is never "Sint-Anna is worth €25M" — it is "somewhere between €20M and €33M, if my beliefs about growth are roughly right." What do you do with a range? You demand a margin of safety: a big gap between price and even the low end of your estimate.

Beginner trap #2 — the precision illusion

A spreadsheet will happily tell you a company is worth "€47.32 per share". The decimals are a costume. Behind them sit two guessed numbers — growth and rate — and you watched with your own hands how one point of either swings the answer by 30–70%.

Rule: valuations are ranges with reasons, never points. Anyone quoting a company's value to the cent — analyst, YouTuber, or your own spreadsheet — has confused output formatting with knowledge.

Beginner trap #3 — paying any price for a great company

"It's an amazing business, so I'll just buy it" — the most expensive sentence in investing after 2021. An amazing business at P/E 60 must deliver near-miracle growth for decades just to justify today's price — greatness is necessary but not sufficient. The company can succeed AND the stock can lose half its value, simply because the promise was even bigger than the success. Great company ≠ great investment. Price decides which.

7. Check yourself

Answer first, then click. Use the formula — it answers four of the six.

You'll receive €1,000 in 5 years. Safe rates are 5%. What is that promise worth today, roughly?

€1,000 ÷ 1.05⁵ ≈ €784. Divide by 1.05 five times. Far money is small money — and the higher the rate, the smaller it gets. This one operation is the atom of all valuation.

Interest rates jump. Why do "exciting growth stocks" fall much harder than boring dividend payers?

Growth stocks' profits live mostly in the far future, and discounting punishes far money hardest when rates rise. In the formula: their (rate − growth) gap is tiny, so a rate increase multiplies through violently (our example: −75% vs −37%). 2022 in one sentence.

A supermarket trades at P/E 8, a software firm at P/E 25. Which is the better buy?

Unknowable from the numbers alone. P/E 8 prices in decline; P/E 25 prices in solid growth. The right question is which promise is wrong: if the supermarket merely stays flat, it's a bargain; if the software grows slower than believed, it's a loss. Multiples are beliefs, not verdicts — decode, then judge the belief.

A stock trades at P/E 60. Using the decoder (8% demanded return), what must you believe to buy it — and is that sane?

1 ÷ 60 ≈ 0.0167, so rate − growth ≈ 1.7%, meaning growth ≈ 6.3% per year, forever — roughly triple the long-run economy, for decades. A handful of companies per generation deliver this. Possible? Yes. The default assumption? Never. You are betting on an exception; size the bet like it.

Your analysis says a company is worth €40–€60 per share. It trades at €52. What do you do?

Nothing. €52 sits inside your honest range — the market's guess and yours are the same guess. You act only with a margin of safety: for this range, interest starts far below €40 (say, under €30). "Do nothing" is the correct output of most valuations you'll ever do.

A stock has a 9% dividend yield — triple the market. Free money?

Almost never. A giant yield usually means the price already crashed because the market expects the dividend to be cut — the yield is high because the "d" in dividend ÷ price is about to shrink. Plus (Lesson 2) a Belgian investor keeps only ~59–70% of gross dividends after taxes. Decode first: what cut is priced in?

8. Key words from this lesson

Discounting — translating future money into today's money by dividing by (1 + rate) per year.
Present value — what a future payment is worth today, after discounting.
Discount rate — the yearly return you demand: safe interest rate + extra for risk.
DCF — valuing a company by discounting all its future profits and adding them up.
The shortcut formula — value = profit ÷ (rate − growth). Fair P/E = 1 ÷ (rate − growth).
Implied expectations — the growth belief hidden inside any price; decode before judging.
Multiple — a price-to-something ruler: P/E, P/B, P/S, EV/EBITDA, dividend yield.
Relative valuation — judging multiples vs peers, own history, and the decoded promise.
Value trap — a stock that looks cheap because the market correctly expects decay.
Margin of safety — acting only when price is far below your lowest honest estimate.
Duration (of a stock) — how far in the future its profits live; farther = more rate-sensitive.
EV — enterprise value: market cap + debt − cash; the price of the whole business.

9. If you remember only three things

  1. Value = profit ÷ (rate − growth). One line holds the whole discipline: future profits, translated to today, with rates as gravity and growth as hope.
  2. Every price is a promise in disguise. Decode the implied growth before judging — "cheap" and "expensive" are statements about beliefs, not numbers.
  3. Valuation outputs a range, so demand a margin of safety. Act only far below your lowest honest number. "Do nothing" is the usual right answer.

10. Homework — decode a real price

Field exercise (~1 hour) — uses your Lesson 3 numbers

Take the Belgian company you analyzed in Lesson 3 (you have its net profit, and from Lesson 1 its P/E).

  • Decode the promise: from its P/E, compute the implied growth at an 8% demanded return: growth ≈ 8% − (1 ÷ P/E × 100). Write it down.
  • Judge the promise: compare that implied growth to the actual revenue growth you computed in Lesson 3. Is the market's belief modest, fair, or heroic? One sentence.
  • Feel the range: compute the value with growth one point higher and one point lower than your best guess. Write the range, not the middle.
  • Hunt a value trap: on any stock screener, find one Belgian or US company with P/E below 8. Read one news story about it and answer: what decline is the market pricing in — and do you see any reason the market is wrong?
  • Play: ten minutes with the value machine sliders. Find the settings that justify a P/E of 60. Look at them. Remember them.

Bring the decoded growth, your range, and your value-trap candidate. From here on, you never look at a P/E the same way again.
