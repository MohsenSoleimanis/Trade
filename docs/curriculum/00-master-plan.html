Ground rules

These come from the debate we ran against the plan. They are the difference between a trading system and an expensive random number generator — every module enforces them.

  • Validation before sophistication. An honest simple backtest beats a dishonest clever one, always. Every hour making the backtest more truthful pays better than an hour making the model fancier.
  • Costs are part of the strategy, not a footnote. Spread, slippage, Belgian TOB, taxes — modeled from day one. A signal that dies after costs was never a signal.
  • Two books, not one. ML needs breadth — it trains on the wide US/pan-European universe. Belgium is a concentrated fundamental/event niche powered by reading filings, not by models.
  • Agents propose, the risk layer disposes. LLM output enters the system only as a timestamped feature. Nothing statistical ever touches position sizing directly.
  • Pre-registered kill criteria. The conditions for stopping a strategy are written before the first live euro, while you have no feelings about it.
  • Expectation reset. The realistic year-one outcome is a disciplined, low-cost portfolio you fully understand plus a research capability in an under-fished niche — not a money printer. That outcome is worth having.

The modules

Track A

Foundations

Before any trading code. Skipping these is why most beginners' systems are wrong in ways they can't see.

M01How markets actually work~2 wks▶

Goal: never be surprised by market mechanics again — fills, auctions, settlement, corporate actions.

You will learn

  • Exchange structure: continuous trading vs opening/closing auctions (Euronext runs auctions that matter for your fills), market makers, who is on the other side of your trade.
  • Order types and the order book: market, limit, stop, MOC/LOC; spread, depth, and why the bid–ask spread is a real cost you pay twice.
  • Settlement (T+1 US, T+2 Europe), what actually happens after a fill, cash vs margin accounts.
  • Corporate actions: splits, dividends, rights issues, spin-offs, delistings — and how each silently corrupts naive price data.
  • Indices, ETFs, and adjusted vs unadjusted prices — what "the price" even means in a dataset.
  • Broker landscape and account setup; tax basics for a Belgian resident trading both markets (TOB, dividend withholding, W-8BEN, the 2026 capital-gains rules).

Deliverable

Open an Interactive Brokers paper account. Place one of every order type on a US and a Brussels-listed stock, and write down for each: what filled, at what price, and why.

Key resources

  • IBKR paper account + its own docs; Euronext trading manual (free PDF); Chan, Quantitative Trading ch. 1–2.

Layers: context for all · Prereq: none

M02Python & tooling for data work~3 wks▶

Goal: pandas fluency good enough that data manipulation never blocks your thinking.

You will learn

  • Environment done right: Python via uv/venv, a real project layout (src/, tests/, config), git basics.
  • pandas deeply: indexing, groupby, merge/join semantics (and how a bad join silently duplicates rows), reshaping, rolling windows, MultiIndex for (date, ticker) panels.
  • Datetime discipline: timezones, exchange calendars, why "today's close" is ambiguous, and holiday handling for two markets at once.
  • NumPy vectorization vs loops; when performance matters and when it doesn't.
  • File formats: why parquet beats CSV for market data; intro to DuckDB for querying years of history on your desktop.
  • Jupyter for exploration vs scripts for pipelines — and why production code never lives in notebooks.

Deliverable

A clean repo that downloads 20 years of daily prices for ~50 US tickers, adjusts them, stores parquet, and computes rolling returns/volatility — with tests that prove the joins and dates are right.

Key resources

  • McKinney, Python for Data Analysis (the pandas author's book); pandas user guide sections on time series & MultiIndex.

Layers: tooling for all · Prereq: none

M03Statistics you will actually use~3 wks▶

Goal: internalize how luck masquerades as skill — the single most protective idea in this whole field.

You will learn

  • Distributions and fat tails: why market returns are not normal and what that breaks (hint: most risk formulas).
  • Sampling error and confidence intervals: how much data you need before a number means anything.
  • Hypothesis testing and p-hacking: what happens to "significance" when you test 200 strategy variants.
  • Regression: assumptions, residuals, and reading a regression table — you'll use this weekly for attribution.
  • Correlation vs causation vs coincidence; spurious correlation in trending time series (the classic trap).
  • Monte Carlo simulation as your everyday reasoning tool.

Deliverable

Simulate 1,000 coin-flip "traders" over 10 years. Find how many look like geniuses (Sharpe > 1) by pure luck. Then compute how many years of live returns you'd need to statistically confirm a real Sharpe 1.0 strategy. Frame the answer above your desk.

Key resources

  • Think Stats (free); López de Prado ch. 1 (why most quant funds fail); "Deflated Sharpe Ratio" paper (skim now, master in M6).

Layers: L5 foundations · Prereq: M2

Track B

The quant core

The heart of the curriculum: data engineering, honest backtesting, signals, ML, and portfolio construction — Layers 0 through 5.

M04Returns, risk & time series~2 wks▶

Goal: own the vocabulary of performance — compute every metric from scratch once, so no library is ever a black box.

You will learn

  • Simple vs log returns, compounding, and the exact places mixing them up corrupts results.
  • Volatility: estimation, annualization (and why √252 is an approximation), volatility clustering.
  • Drawdowns, Sharpe, Sortino, Calmar — what each hides, and Sharpe's sensitivity to measurement window.
  • Stationarity and autocorrelation: why price levels are nearly unpredictable but cross-sectional ranks can be predictable.
  • Benchmarks and excess returns: performance is always relative to something you could have bought passively.

Deliverable

Your own metrics library (returns, vol, drawdown, Sharpe, rolling versions) built from scratch with tests — this exact code gets reused by every later module.

Key resources

  • Chan, Quantitative Trading ch. 3; Ilmanen, Expected Returns intro chapters (why anything earns a return at all).

Layers: L5 · Prereq: M3

M05Data engineering: build Layers 0–1~4 wks▶

Goal: build the foundation everything sits on. This is where 60% of real quant work lives, and where every fake backtest is born.

You will learn

  • Point-in-time data: the two-timestamp design (when a fact is about vs when it became knowable) and why a Q1 earnings number doesn't exist on March 31.
  • Survivorship bias: building a universe table that includes delisted names, and measuring how badly its absence inflates results.
  • Corporate-action adjustment done correctly (back-adjustment, dividend treatment) and validating it against known splits.
  • Source landscape: free tiers (yfinance, Stooq, EDGAR) vs paid EOD vendors (Norgate, Sharadar, EODHD) — what each is trustworthy for.
  • Storage and pipelines: parquet + DuckDB schema for prices/fundamentals/universe; idempotent daily ingestion jobs; automated data-quality checks (gaps, spikes, stale series).

Deliverable

Your production Layer 0–1: universe table, adjusted price store, ingestion pipeline with quality checks — the actual foundation of your system, not an exercise.

Key resources

  • López de Prado ch. 2 (financial data structures); Norgate/Sharadar docs on survivorship; your own M2 repo, upgraded.

Layers: L0 L1 · Prereq: M2, M4 · Runs alongside M14

M06Honest backtesting~4 wks▶

Goal: build one backtest you can defend line by line against a hostile senior. The most important module in the curriculum.

You will learn

  • The seven deadly sins: lookahead, survivorship, ignored costs, overfitting, data snooping, single-regime testing, capacity blindness — with a live demonstration of each inflating the same strategy.
  • Event-driven vs vectorized backtests, and the exact bug class each hides.
  • Cost modeling: spread, slippage, commissions, Belgian TOB — and turnover as the tax on every idea.
  • Walk-forward testing; in-sample vs out-of-sample discipline; keeping an experiment ledger that counts every variant you ever tried.
  • Deflated Sharpe ratio: correcting your best result for how many things you tried.

Deliverable

A 12-1 month momentum backtest on your survivorship-free US universe, with full costs, walk-forward, and a written defense of every assumption. This becomes your permanent baseline.

Key resources

  • López de Prado ch. 11–14 (backtesting dangers); Bailey & López de Prado, "The Deflated Sharpe Ratio"; vectorbt / zipline-reloaded source as reference implementations.

Layers: L5 · Prereq: M5

M07Factors & signals~3 wks▶

Goal: know the signals with a century of evidence behind them — the economic raw material your ML will combine.

You will learn

  • The canon: momentum, value, quality/profitability, low-volatility, size — the evidence, and the economic story for why each exists and who is on the other side.
  • Alpha decay: McLean & Pontiff's result that published anomalies lose 30–50% post-publication, and how to calibrate expectations for anything you read.
  • Signal hygiene: z-scoring, ranking, winsorization, sector neutralization, information coefficient (IC) as the unit of signal quality.
  • Earnings revisions, insider/ownership signals, and event-driven signals — the mid-horizon menu.
  • Combining signals: why a simple average of good signals is brutally hard to beat.

Deliverable

A cross-sectional factor study: momentum, value, and quality on your US universe — IC, decile spreads, decay curves — through your M6 harness.

Key resources

  • Gray & Vogel, Quantitative Momentum; Open Source Asset Pricing (Chen & Zimmermann) dataset; AQR & Alpha Architect factor publications.

Layers: L2 L3 · Prereq: M6

M08ML for cross-sectional ranking — Book A~5 wks▶

Goal: use ML the way funds actually do — to combine signals efficiently on a wide universe — and prove whether it beats your simple baseline.

You will learn

  • Problem formulation: predict cross-sectional rank next month, never price — and why breadth (thousands of names) is what gives the model statistical power.
  • LightGBM on tabular features: training, regularization, and why gradient-boosted trees beat deep learning at this data scale.
  • Purged & embargoed cross-validation: why standard k-fold leaks the future in finance, and the correct alternative.
  • Feature importance that survives scrutiny: permutation importance, SHAP, and detecting when the model learned noise.
  • The verdict protocol: ML model vs M7's equal-weight signal average, after costs, deflated — with a pre-committed decision rule.
  • Study of Microsoft qlib: how a real open-source quant ML platform structures exactly this pipeline.

Deliverable

A LightGBM ranker on the wide universe evaluated against the M7 baseline through identical costs and validation — plus your written verdict on whether it earns its complexity.

Key resources

  • López de Prado ch. 6–9 (CV & feature importance); qlib repo + docs; Kaggle time-series CV write-ups (good and bad, told apart with your M6 eyes).

Layers: L3 Book A · Prereq: M7

M09Portfolio, risk & attribution~3 wks▶

Goal: turn rankings into positions deterministically, and build the layer that tells you whether your "alpha" is just beta in a costume.

You will learn

  • Position sizing: equal weight vs volatility targeting vs risk parity; why Kelly is a ceiling, never a target.
  • Constraints as risk management: max weight per name, sector caps, turnover limits, liquidity/capacity caps (hard caps on thin Belgian names).
  • Transaction-cost-aware rebalancing: no-trade bands, and trading only when the expected edge clears the cost hurdle.
  • The attribution regression: your returns vs a handful of factor ETFs, monthly — the standing "why not just buy the ETF?" test.
  • Capital allocation between Book A (systematic) and Book B (Belgian niche) as an explicit, revisable decision.

Deliverable

Your Layer 4: rankings in, orders out, all constraints enforced — plus the monthly attribution report as an automated script.

Key resources

  • Grinold & Kahn, Active Portfolio Management (the fundamental law: IR ≈ IC × √breadth); skfolio; Carhart four-factor model as the attribution starting point.

Layers: L4 L5 · Prereq: M8

Track C

Engineering & operations

Where the backtest becomes a system that runs unattended every day — and where retail projects usually die.

M10Execution: the paper-trading harness~3 wks▶

Goal: the full pipeline runs daily against an IBKR paper account — and you understand that this verifies plumbing, not alpha.

You will learn

  • The IBKR API (ib_insync-style workflow): contracts, symbol mapping across US and Euronext (a classic silent-failure zone), order placement, fills.
  • Order management: state machines for order lifecycle, partial fills, retries, idempotency (never send the same order twice after a crash).
  • Reconciliation: your system's belief of positions vs the broker's truth, checked every day.
  • Euronext specifics: auction participation, tick sizes, and why limit orders are non-negotiable on thin Belgian names.
  • Scheduling the daily run on Windows, with clean failure behavior when a data source is down.

Deliverable

The complete loop — data → features → model → portfolio → paper orders — running unattended daily, with reconciliation and failure alerts.

Key resources

  • IBKR API docs + paper account; ib_insync (or its maintained successor) examples; Euronext trading manual auction sections.

Layers: L6 · Prereq: M9

M11Monitoring, governance & kill criteria~2 wks▶

Goal: the system watches itself, and the rules for stopping it exist in writing before you have feelings about it.

You will learn

  • Data health monitoring: stale feeds, gaps, spikes, schema drift — a system silently fed garbage will happily trade garbage.
  • Live-vs-backtest divergence tracking: expected vs realized fills, costs, and returns, with alert thresholds.
  • Feature and prediction drift: detecting when the world your model learned no longer exists.
  • Kill criteria: pre-registering drawdown limits, divergence limits, and data-quality conditions that halt a book — and the runbook for what happens next.
  • Logging and audit: reconstructing any past decision the system made, months later.

Deliverable

A monitoring dashboard + alerting, and your signed one-page kill-criteria document for each book.

Key resources

  • Your own M10 logs as the dataset; SRE-style runbook patterns; the M3 coin-flip result, re-read.

Layers: L7 · Prereq: M10

Track D

The agentic layer

LLM agents as research infrastructure — readable from Module 6 onward, in parallel with Track B. Agents propose; the risk layer disposes.

M12LLMs for finance work~3 wks▶

Goal: treat an LLM like any other model — measured, evaluated, and never trusted on vibes.

You will learn

  • LLM APIs properly: structured outputs (JSON schemas), tool use, batching, cost control.
  • Extraction over generation: pulling facts from filings into typed fields, with citations back to the source text.
  • Evaluation as a first-class step: hand-label a gold set, measure extraction accuracy, and set acceptance thresholds — an unevaluated LLM feature is a rumor, not a feature.
  • Hallucination containment: constrained outputs, "answer only from the document," and refusing to emit a value when unsure.
  • The feature contract: LLM output enters Layer 2 as a timestamped feature row and passes the same validation as any price ratio.

Deliverable

An EDGAR filing extractor (e.g., 8-K events → structured fields) with a measured accuracy score on a hand-labeled gold set.

Key resources

  • Anthropic API docs (structured outputs, tool use); SEC EDGAR full-text search & APIs; your M3 statistics, applied to your own eval set.

Layers: L2 L8 · Prereq: M2 · Parallel from M6

M13Research agents — Layer 8~4 wks▶

Goal: build the research capability that is your actual edge — including the deep-source sweeps you asked for on day one.

You will learn

  • The literature-sweep agent: citation-graph walking (Semantic Scholar, SSRN, arXiv q-fin) plus forums, scoring every source against the evaluation checklist (out-of-sample? costs? experiment count disclosed?) — substance over polish, automated.
  • The filings pipeline for Belgium: NBB Balance Sheet Centre and FSMA disclosures in Dutch/French → structured features. The under-fished corner where a language-capable solo player has a real advantage.
  • News and event monitors with strict deduplication and timestamping (an event is only tradable if you know when it became knowable).
  • Agent architecture: orchestration, retries, human-review queues for low-confidence output, and the hard wall — no write access below Layer 4, ever.
  • Measuring the research layer itself: are its features earning IC? Are its reading lists actually good? Agents get attribution too.

Deliverable

Two running agents: the literature sweep producing ranked, red-flag-annotated reading lists; and the Belgian filings pipeline producing timestamped features for Book B.

Key resources

  • Claude Agent SDK; Semantic Scholar API; NBB Balance Sheet Centre & FSMA portals; your M12 evaluation discipline.

Layers: L8 L2 · Prereq: M12

M15Belgium deep dive — Book B~3 wks▶

Goal: turn Belgium from "my home market" into a researched niche: small, illiquid, under-covered — and yours.

You will learn

  • Euronext Brussels mechanics: BEL 20/Mid/Small indices, the continuous vs double-auction segments (many small caps trade by auction only), tick sizes, liquidity reality.
  • The cost stack that shapes everything: TOB per transaction, 30% dividend withholding, the 2026 capital-gains regime — verified against current FSMA/FOD Finance rules, not blog posts.
  • Primary sources nobody mines: NBB Balance Sheet Centre (every Belgian company's accounts), FSMA shareholding notifications, KBO/BCE registry, Euronext notices.
  • Why backtests on thin names are hints, not forecasts: your order is the market; sizing and limit-order discipline as the only honest response.
  • Book B's process: screening → filing deep-reads (LLM-assisted, human-judged) → a concentrated watchlist with written theses and position caps.

Deliverable

Belgian universe with liquidity tiers in Layer 0; the filings inventory wired to your M13 pipeline; a first Book B watchlist of 5–10 names with one-page theses.

Key resources

  • Euronext Brussels rulebook; NBB & FSMA portals; current Belgian tax guidance (verify — rules changed in 2026).
