What a Stock Is and Why Prices Move

This lesson answers three questions. What exactly do you buy, when you buy a share? Who decides the price? And why does the price change? Understand these three deeply, and you are already ahead of most beginners.

reading: ~45 minhomework: ~1 hrprerequisite: noneinteractive: yes — press the buttons

1. A share is a piece of a real business

The idea in one sentenceA share is a small piece of a real company — and when the company earns money, part of that money belongs to you.

Someone says: "I bought Apple stock." What did they actually buy? Not a symbol on a screen. Not a bet. They bought a small piece of the real company Apple — a legal piece of its factories, its brand, its cash, and its future profits.

It works like this. A company cuts its ownership into equal pieces. Each piece is called a share. Small example: a company is cut into 100 shares. You buy 1 share. Now you own 1% of that company. If the company earns €1,000,000 profit this year, then €10,000 of that profit belongs to you — because 1% of €1,000,000 is €10,000.

How does that profit reach you? Only two ways exist:

  • Dividend — the company sends part of the profit to the owners as cash. Real money arrives in your broker account, usually once or twice per year.
  • Reinvestment — the company keeps the profit and uses it to grow: new stores, new products. You receive nothing today. But your piece is now a piece of a bigger company. That is what pushes the value up over time.
Story — the bakery

Your neighbor owns a bakery. Every year it earns €100,000 profit, very reliably. She offers to sell you 10% of the bakery. What price is fair?

The bakery math — step by step
  1. The bakery earns €100,000 profit per year.
  2. You would own 10%. So your part of the profit is €100,000 × 10% = €10,000 per year.
  3. If you pay €50,000: you get your money back in €50,000 ÷ €10,000 = 5 years. After that, everything is gain. Great deal.
  4. If you pay €100,000: money back in 10 years. Reasonable.
  5. If you pay €500,000: money back in 50 years. Almost certainly a bad deal.

The stock market uses the exact same idea, with two special words:

From the bakery to a real stock — the two words you'll see every day
  1. EPS ("earnings per share") = the company's profit, divided by the number of shares. Example: profit €5,000,000, shares 1,000,000 → EPS = €5. Meaning: each share "earned" €5 this year.
  2. P/E ("price to earnings") = the share price, divided by EPS. Example: price €100, EPS €5 → P/E = 20. Meaning: you are paying 20 years of today's profit. Exactly like the bakery.
In simple wordsP/E answers: "how many years of profit am I paying for?" A high P/E means the market believes profit will grow (you pay extra for the future). A low P/E usually means the market believes profit will fall. So high is not always expensive, and low is not always cheap — the price always contains a belief about the future.
Beginner trap #1 — a "cheap" share price does not exist

A €5 share is not cheaper than a €500 share. Why? The share price depends on how many pieces the company was cut into. Cut one €500 share into 100 pieces, and each piece costs €5 — but nothing about the company changed.

The number that matters is market cap: share price × number of shares = the value of the whole company. Always ask: "is the whole company worth that?" Never: "is €5 a low number?"

2. Who decides the price? Nobody — it's a live negotiation

The idea in one sentenceThe stock price is simply the last deal between one buyer and one seller — nothing more.

Nobody decides that Microsoft costs $420. There is no committee, no government office. The price on your screen only means: this is the price of the most recent deal.

Think of a fruit market. Sellers shout: "tomatoes, €3 per kilo!" Buyers answer: "I'll pay €2.80, not more." No deal happens until one side gives in. When someone finally says "fine, €2.90" — that deal becomes "the price of tomatoes" for a moment. The stock market is exactly this, but a computer keeps everyone's offers in two lists.

That computer is the exchange (Nasdaq and NYSE in the US, Euronext for Brussels). Your broker is the company that sends your orders to it. And the two lists of offers together are called the order book. Here is a real one, live. First learn to read it, then press the button:

  • Every row is one price.
  • Red bars (top) = people who want to sell, and the price they demand.
  • Green bars (bottom) = people who want to buy, and the price they offer.
  • The longer the bar, the more shares are offered at that price.
  • The empty space in the middle = prices where nobody agrees. That gap is called the spread.

Did you press it? Here is exactly what happened, in slow motion:

Your order, step by step
  1. You asked for 600 shares "at any available price". This is called a market order.
  2. The cheapest seller only had 300 shares, at €20.05. You took all of them: 300 × €20.05 = €6,015.
  3. You still needed 300 more. The next seller wanted €20.10: 300 × €20.10 = €6,030.
  4. Total: €12,045 for 600 shares. Average price: €12,045 ÷ 600 = €20.075 per share.
  5. The fair middle price was €20.00. At €20.00, 600 shares would cost €12,000. You paid €45 extra. That €45 is the hidden cost of trading — no bank statement will ever show it as a "fee".
  6. And notice the last price: it is now €20.10. The chart went up. But there was no news. The company did not change. You were the only reason. Remember this every time you see a price wiggle.
Beginner trap #2 — always check the volume before you buy

Volume = how many shares are traded per day. Multiply by the price and you get the money flowing through the stock daily. Apple: about $10 billion per day. A small Belgian stock: sometimes only €50,000 per day.

On that small stock, even €5,000 of buying visibly pushes the price up (like your button just did). And when you want to sell in a hurry — for example in a panic, when everyone sells — there may simply be no buyers near the current price. Before buying anything, ask: "could I sell my whole position in one day, without destroying the price?"

3. Why prices move: the surprise machine

The idea in one sentencePrices do not move because news is good or bad. Prices move when the news is different from what people expected.

Start with something familiar. Your friend always scores 9/10 on every exam. Today he scores 7/10. Is 7/10 a good grade? Yes. But everyone is disappointed — because they expected 9. Now the reverse: a weak student who always fails suddenly scores 6/10. A worse grade than your friend's! But everyone celebrates. The reaction follows the surprise, not the grade itself.

Stock prices work exactly like this, and here is why. If everyone believes a company's profit will grow 25% next year, they don't wait — they buy the stock today. All that buying pushes the price up today. So by the time the real results arrive, the 25% growth is already inside the price. People say the news is "priced in".

Then the real number arrives, and only one thing matters: is it better or worse than the 25% everyone assumed?

This idea has a famous name: the efficient market idea. It says: everything that everyone knows is already inside the price. In its extreme version ("nobody can ever beat the market") it is too strong — later lessons show the known exceptions. But as your default assumption, it protects you, because three practical rules follow from it:

  • News you read on a website is already in the price. Computers traded it within milliseconds, hours before you saw it.
  • Facts that everybody knows ("this company is great", "AI is the future") are already in the price. Being right about what everyone knows earns you nothing.
  • To beat the market you need one of three things: information others don't have, an interpretation others didn't make, or patience others can't afford. There is no fourth option.

So what pushes prices, hour by hour and year by year?

Several forces: profit surprises (the machine above), interest rates and economic news, flows (big funds that must buy or sell for reasons that have nothing to do with the company — for example an index fund rebalancing), sentiment (waves of group greed and group fear), and — most of the time — pure randomness, like your 600-share order. 4. Who is selling to you, when you buy?

The idea in one sentenceEvery euro you win in trading comes from another person — so before every trade, ask who is on the other side and why you would beat them.

Trading is not shopping. In a shop, the cashier is neutral. In trading, when you buy, a real counterparty sells to you — and one of you will turn out to be wrong. So it is worth knowing who sits at the table:

  • Market makers — companies whose computers stand in the order book all day, on both sides. They sell to you at €20.05 and buy from you at €19.95, pocketing the spread thousands of times per day. They are why you can always trade instantly. On small Belgian stocks, few of them bother — that's why the spread there is wide.
  • Institutions — pension funds and insurers moving huge amounts slowly. Important: they often must trade (new pension money arrives, a fund must copy an index) no matter the price. A trader who must trade can lose to you without being stupid — they are rare and valuable counterparties.
  • Quant funds — the professional computer traders. They dominate all fast patterns. Competing with them on speed is hopeless — one more reason our plan uses long horizons.
  • Retail — private people. Studies keep finding the same result: people who trade often lose against the market, mostly by paying the spread again and again, and by buying whatever is exciting this week.

So, the question to ask before every single trade, for the rest of your life: "the person selling to me — do they know something I don't, or do they need something I don't? Why is my side of this deal the smart side?" If you have no answer, your trade is a coin flip — minus costs.

5. "The market" — indices and ETFs

The idea in one sentenceAn ETF lets you buy hundreds of companies in one purchase, almost for free — and it is the score you must beat before your trading means anything.

When the news says "the market went up today," it means an index: a list of stocks with a formula. The S&P 500 is a list of the 500 biggest US companies. The BEL 20 is a list of the 20 biggest in Brussels — careful: it is dominated by a few giants (AB InBev, KBC, UCB…), so it is less diversified than "20 companies" sounds.

You cannot buy a list. But an ETF is a product that contains all the stocks from the list, and it trades like a normal share. One purchase = 500 companies. Cost: as low as ~0.1% per year. Two reasons this matters enormously to you:

  • The ETF is your score to beat. A world-stock ETF earned roughly 6–8% per year on average, over long periods, for zero effort. So if your active trading made 8% in a year when the ETF made 12%, your trading lost you 4%. Every result must be compared to this free alternative — this exact comparison will later be built into your system as an automatic report.
  • The ETF is the honest default. Money that has not yet earned the right to be actively traded — through demonstrated, measured skill — belongs in a broad ETF. Professionals think this way. Gamblers don't.

Beginner trap #3 — trading a lot feels like progress. It isn't.

Look at the cost chart in section 2 one more time. Every single trade pays: spread + slippage + TOB tax + commission. The feeling of "doing something" is the most expensive feeling in finance. The market pays for being right — never for being active.

7. Check yourself

Try to answer first. Then click to see the answer. If an answer surprises you, re-read that section — these six questions cover the ideas everything else will be built on.

A share costs €3. Your friend says: "so cheap, it can easily double!" What is wrong with that?

The price of one share means nothing by itself — it depends on how many shares exist. A €3 share × 10 billion shares = a €30 billion company. For the share to double, the market must decide the whole company is worth €60 billion. The right question is never "is €3 low?" — it is "is the whole company cheap compared to its profits?"

A medicine company announces: our new drug is approved! The stock barely moves. Why?

Everyone already expected the approval, so it was already inside the price ("priced in") — people bought weeks ago, and their buying already pushed the price up back then. No surprise → no move. Funny enough, a rejection would have moved the price a lot, because that would have been the surprise.

You want to put €10,000 into a Belgian small-cap that trades €40,000 per day, with a 2% spread. What problems are waiting for you?

Your order is a quarter of a full day's trading — buying will push the price up against you, like the BUY 600 button did. Selling later, especially in a panic when everyone sells, could take days or force you to accept bad prices. And the costs: ~2% spread + ~0.7% TOB before anything even happens. Small stocks demand small positions and lots of patience.

Your stock went up 1.5% today. What did you learn about the company?

Almost nothing. Look at the "1 day" bar in the horizon chart: daily moves are mostly random noise. Unless there was real news, a 1.5% day carries no information. Beginners who judge their decisions by daily moves teach themselves wrong lessons with great confidence.

When you buy, who is probably selling to you — and why does it matter?

Usually a market maker (earning the spread from you) or a big institution's algorithm. It matters because trading is person-against-person: to win repeatedly, you must know why the other side is losing to you — they are forced to trade, or slower, or thinking in a different time period. "I feel it will go up" has no answer to this question.

You made 9% last year with active trading. A world ETF made 12%. How good was your year?

Bad. The free, zero-effort alternative made 12% — your work and risk produced 3% less than doing nothing. "I made money" is not the test. "I beat the ETF" is the test. (Later we refine this: 9% with much lower risk can still be respectable — that's Lesson 6.)

8. Key words from this lesson

Share — one piece of ownership of a company, with a right to its profits.
Market cap — share price × number of shares = value of the whole company.
EPS — profit ÷ number of shares = how much one share "earned" this year.
P/E — price ÷ EPS = how many years of profit you pay. The bakery question.
Dividend — part of the profit paid to owners as cash.
Order book — the live lists of everyone who wants to buy or sell, and at what price.
Bid / Ask / Spread — best buyer / best seller / the gap between them (a cost you pay).
Market order — "fill me now at any price". Fast, but pays spread + slippage.
Slippage — the price moving against you because your own order ate the cheap offers.
Volume — shares traded per day (× price = € per day). The measure of liquidity.
Priced in — already expected by everyone, therefore already inside the price.
Index / ETF — a list of stocks / a buyable product containing that whole list.

9. If you remember only three things

  1. A share is a piece of a real business. Its price is someone's answer to "how many years of this company's profit am I paying?" (that's the P/E). Think in market cap, never in price-per-share.
  2. Prices move on surprise, not on news. What everyone already knows is already inside the price. To profit, you need something others don't have: information, interpretation, or patience.
  3. Every trade costs money even when nothing moves — spread, slippage, TOB tax. Trading rarely and holding long is not laziness; it is an advantage, especially in Belgium.

10. Homework — touch the real thing

Field exercise (~1 hour, no money involved)

Pick two stocks: one huge US company you know (e.g. Microsoft or Coca-Cola), and one Belgian mid/small company (e.g. Lotus Bakeries, Melexis, or Kinepolis). For each one, on Yahoo Finance (free) or your broker:

  • Write down: market cap, P/E, dividend yield, daily volume, and the bid–ask spread if you can see it.
  • Compute the money volume: volume × price. How many times bigger is the US one?
  • Answer in one sentence each: Could I invest €5,000 without moving the price? What does this P/E say the market believes about future profit? How much % would one round trip cost me here (spread + TOB)?
  • Bonus: find one news headline about the Belgian stock from the last month. Write down what the market probably expected before that news. Then check: did the price reaction match the surprise machine?
