De Waag — your personal quant workstation

"De Waag" — the weigh house — is the old Flemish building where merchants weighed goods before trading them. This is a production-grade personal trading system that weighs before it trades: research workbench, risk engine, backtest lab, paper-trading desk, and an AI research floor — with a teaching layer that shows the why behind every number on every screen.

7 modules 9 layers implemented 7 build phases ≈ 6–9 months part-time local-first · paper-first · your data

1. What this is — and the honest frame

Product definitionDe Waag is the class of software that real firms build and sell — a research platform + order/risk management + analytics (think Bloomberg terminal, FactSet, a fund's internal risk console) — scaled to one intelligent owner, wired to your Risk Constitution, and instrumented to teach while you use it.
The honest frame — read before anything else

Companies that "developed trading systems for years and sell them" sell exactly three kinds of things: (1) research & data platforms (Bloomberg, FactSet — $25k/year/seat), (2) execution & risk infrastructure (order management systems), and (3) backtesting/analytics engines (QuantConnect, in-house at funds). Nobody sane sells a machine that prints money — anything sold as one fails your Lesson 7 lie detector instantly (a real edge sold cheap = no edge).

So De Waag is those three real things, built for you. Its promise is professional: better decisions, enforced discipline, honest measurement, compounding research skill — not predictions. The alpha, if any, comes from where our master plan said: patience, quality/value discipline, and the under-fished Belgian niche. The system is the factory, not the gold.

Product principles (every design decision below follows from these)

  • Weigh before trading. No order ticket exists without a thesis, a size computed from the risk budget, and a cost preview. The software physically enforces Lessons 2, 4, 6.
  • Teach the why, everywhere. Every metric, every screen, every block has a "why?" drawer: the logic, the lesson it comes from, the trap it prevents. Using the system is the practice you asked for.
  • Deterministic core, advisory AI. Agents read, extract, summarize, and propose. Only the deterministic risk layer touches sizes and orders. (Master plan, Layer 8 rule.)
  • Benchmark honesty built in. The dashboard's first number is always you-vs-the-ETF. If the system can't beat the free alternative, it must say so to your face, monthly.
  • Local-first and private. Runs on your machine; your data, theses, and mistakes stay yours. No cloud dependency for the core.
  • Paper until proven. Live trading unlocks only when pre-registered criteria are met (months of paper history + constitution compliance). The system enforces its own kill criteria.

3. Architecture & stack — every choice with its why

ChoicePickWhy this and not the alternatives
Language (engine)Python 3.12The entire quant ecosystem lives here (pandas, DuckDB, LightGBM, backtesting libs). Any other language means rebuilding tools professionals already battle-tested. Speed is irrelevant at daily horizon.
StorageParquet + DuckDBNo database server to babysit; files you can open and inspect (debuggable = trustable); DuckDB queries 20 years of prices in milliseconds. Postgres would add ops burden for zero benefit at this scale. Schema enforces the two-timestamp point-in-time rule from day one.
API layerFastAPIOne Python process serves both the engine and the UI — no microservice theater. Typed request/response models double as documentation you'll learn from.
FrontendReact + TypeScript + ViteThe industry standard you'd meet in any fintech; component model fits the module/why-drawer design; TS types catch mistakes a beginner would otherwise ship. Charts: TradingView's open-source lightweight-charts for prices, custom SVG (like your lessons) for the rest.
BrokerIBKR + ib_asyncOnly retail broker with a real API covering both US and Euronext Brussels (master plan requirement), free paper mode, and the same API later for live — nothing to rewrite when trust is earned.
AgentsClaude API (Sonnet default, Opus/Fable for deep reads)Structured outputs with JSON schemas make agent answers machine-checkable; Dutch/French filing comprehension is exactly the frontier-model strength your Book B niche needs. Every extractor ships with a hand-labeled gold set and a measured accuracy score — an unevaluated LLM feature stays out of the vault (Lesson 12 discipline).
Data (start)yfinance + Stooq + EDGAR + NBBFree tiers are enough to build and learn on; the vault's schema is provider-agnostic, so upgrading later (Norgate/EODHD for survivorship-free history, ~€30/mo) is a config change, not a rebuild.
SchedulerWindows Task Scheduler → supervisorStart with what your machine has; graduate to a small supervising process when jobs multiply. A €5/mo VPS only if you ever want it running while the PC sleeps.
Testingpytest + data-quality gatesProfessional-grade means: the sizing math has unit tests, and a data check that fails blocks the pipeline (Lesson: a system silently fed garbage happily trades garbage).

4. The teaching layer — how the system trains you while you use it

The contractEvery screen answers three questions on demand: what is this number, why does it matter (which lesson, which trap), and what would a professional check next. You asked to be shown the logic — the logic is a first-class feature.
  • Why-drawers everywhere. Every metric and every block/warning has the ⓘ drawer you saw in the mockups: definition → lesson link → the mistake it prevents. Content comes from your curriculum files, so the app and the lessons stay one system.
  • Gates as teachers. When the ticket blocks a market order on a thin Belgian stock, the drawer replays Lesson 2's €45 simulation with this stock's real spread. Blocks are never silent — each one is a micro-lesson at the exact moment you're motivated to learn it.
  • The decision journal. Every order stores its thesis, the "I'm wrong if", and the why-now. Every closed position generates a review card: outcome vs thesis, luck vs skill self-rating. After 20 trades the journal page shows your personal bias statistics — Lesson 8, measured on you.
  • Tutor mode. A toggle that adds Claude-generated walk-throughs on any page: "explain this company's dashboard like Lesson 3" — using your actual portfolio as the teaching material. Off by default; never between you and the raw numbers.
  • The experiment ledger. The Backtest Lab refuses to show you a result without logging the attempt — because the deadliest lie in quant is forgetting how many things you tried (coming in Part 2, Lesson: deflated Sharpe).

5. Build roadmap — seven phases, each one usable and each one a lesson

Professional software ships in increments that are each independently valuable. Same here — and each phase is the practical half of a Part 2 module, so building De Waag is the rest of your curriculum:

PHASE 0

Foundation

~1 week

Fresh git repo dewaag/, Python + Node toolchain, project skeleton, config system, and your risk-constitution.yaml — the Lesson 6 document, now as machine-enforceable config.

You learn: professional project structure, git, why config > hardcoding. Deliverable: the app boots and shows your constitution.
PHASE 1

Data Vault (Layers 0–1)

~3–4 weeks

Universe tables (US large caps + all Euronext Brussels), daily price ingestion, corporate-action adjustment, EDGAR + NBB document fetchers, the two-timestamp schema, and data-quality gates that block on gaps/spikes.

  • Deliverable: 20 years of clean history queryable in milliseconds; a daily job that keeps it fresh unattended.
You learn: point-in-time discipline, survivorship bias hands-on, why data engineering is 60% of real quant work. (= master plan M05/M14.)
PHASE 2

Research Workbench

~3 weeks

Company pages: price chart, auto-computed Lesson 3 toolkit (margins, ROE, debt, cash conversion — with trend arrows), the Lesson 4 valuation decoder with sliders on real numbers, quote decoding, why-drawers throughout.

  • Deliverable: open any of your stocks and do a full L3+L4 analysis in ten minutes instead of ninety.
You practice: statements and valuation on every company you ever look at, forever. The homework you owe me becomes a two-click habit.
PHASE 3

Risk Console + Trading Desk

~3–4 weeks

The backwards position sizer, exposure/drawdown-in-euros meters, constitution gates, the thesis-required order ticket with cost preview, IBKR paper connection, nightly reconciliation, Euronext auction awareness.

  • Deliverable: your paper portfolio runs through De Waag with every Lesson 2 + 6 rule physically enforced.
You practice: sizing, order types, costs — on every single trade, with the software as referee. (= M10 first half.)
PHASE 4

Backtest Lab

~4–5 weeks

The honest engine: walk-forward, mandatory cost model (incl. TOB), the experiment ledger, deflated statistics, and the first canonical test — the 12-1 momentum baseline on the US universe.

  • Deliverable: a backtest you can defend line by line, and the tooling to test every idea you'll ever have.
You learn: the seven deadly sins of backtesting by building the defenses yourself. (= M06, the most important module of Part 2.)
PHASE 5

Screener + Book A signals

~4 weeks

Factor lenses (quality/value/momentum) as transparent, inspectable screens over the vault; signal combination; monthly rebalance proposals for the systematic sleeve — every proposal passing through Risk Console gates. LightGBM ranking comes later, only after the simple average of signals sets the bar (master-plan verdict protocol).

  • Deliverable: a monthly Book A proposal email: "here's the target portfolio, here's why, here's the cost to get there."
You learn: factors as living code instead of blog words; why simple baselines are brutally hard to beat. (= M07, M08 later.)
PHASE 6

Agent Floor

~4–5 weeks

Claude-powered pipelines: EDGAR 8-K event extractor (with gold-set accuracy score), the NBB/FSMA Dutch-French filing reader for Book B, the literature sweeper that scores sources with your four-question checklist, and news monitors. All output lands in the vault as timestamped features and readable briefs with citations.

  • Deliverable: a morning brief — "what happened in your universe, what's worth reading, here are the extracted facts with sources."
You learn: LLM engineering with evaluation discipline — the skill that makes Book B scale beyond your reading hours. (= M12, M13.)
PHASE 7

Governance & the long run

~2–3 weeks, then forever

Monitoring dashboards (data health, live-vs-expected divergence), the kill-criteria board, monthly attribution report vs the ETF (the "why does this system deserve to exist" page), and the live-trading unlock checklist — which stays locked until months of paper history satisfy the pre-registered criteria.

You learn: operations, the unglamorous 20% that separates systems that survive from projects that rot. (= M11.)
